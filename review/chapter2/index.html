üî¨ Section 1: Scientific Method & Atomic Theory

The Scientific Method

The scientific method is a systematic approach to understanding the natural world through observation, hypothesis formation, experimentation, and conclusion. This process led to the development of atomic theory, one of chemistry's most fundamental concepts.

Key Concepts:

  • The scientific method involves observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and conclusion
  • Modern atomic theory states that all matter is composed of atoms
  • Atoms of the same element are identical; atoms of different elements are different
  • Atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds
  • Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms, not creation or destruction
  • John Dalton developed the first modern atomic theory in the early 1800s
  • Atomic theory explains the law of conservation of mass and law of definite proportions

Key Principles of Atomic Theory:

1. All matter is made of atoms (indivisible particles)

2. Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties

3. Compounds are formed by combining atoms in fixed ratios

4. Chemical reactions rearrange atoms but don't create or destroy them

Example: Applying Atomic Theory

Problem: If 2 atoms of hydrogen combine with 1 atom of oxygen to form water, how many oxygen atoms are needed to react with 10 hydrogen atoms?

Solution: Using the fixed ratio (2:1), we need 10 √∑ 2 = 5 oxygen atoms. This demonstrates that atoms combine in whole-number ratios.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note:

While Dalton's atomic theory was groundbreaking, we now know that atoms CAN be divided into subatomic particles (protons, neutrons, electrons). However, atoms remain indivisible in chemical reactions.

‚öõÔ∏è Section 2: Subatomic Particles

Subatomic Particles

Atoms are composed of three fundamental subatomic particles: protons (positive charge), neutrons (no charge), and electrons (negative charge). These particles determine an atom's identity, mass, and chemical behavior.

Key Concepts:

  • Protons have a positive charge (+1) and mass of approximately 1 amu
  • Neutrons have no charge (0) and mass of approximately 1 amu
  • Electrons have a negative charge (-1) and negligible mass (~1/2000 amu)
  • The nucleus contains protons and neutrons; electrons orbit in the space around the nucleus
  • Most of an atom's mass is concentrated in the tiny, dense nucleus
  • Most of an atom's volume is empty space where electrons are found
  • Atoms are electrically neutral because # protons = # electrons

Particle Properties Summary:

Proton: Charge = +1 | Mass = 1.0073 amu | Location = Nucleus

Neutron: Charge = 0 | Mass = 1.0087 amu | Location = Nucleus

Electron: Charge = -1 | Mass = 0.00055 amu | Location = Outside nucleus

Atomic Number (Z) = Number of protons

Mass Number (A) = Protons + Neutrons

Example: Counting Subatomic Particles

Problem: Carbon-12 has an atomic number of 6 and mass number of 12. How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does it have?

Solution:

‚Ä¢ Protons = Atomic number = 6

‚Ä¢ Electrons = Protons (in neutral atom) = 6

‚Ä¢ Neutrons = Mass number - Atomic number = 12 - 6 = 6

üí° Key Insight:

The atomic number defines the element! All carbon atoms have 6 protons. If an atom has a different number of protons, it's a different element entirely.

üìä Section 3: The Periodic Table

The Periodic Table

The periodic table is an organized arrangement of elements based on their atomic number and recurring chemical properties. It's one of chemistry's most powerful tools, allowing us to predict element behavior and find patterns in nature.

Key Concepts:

  • Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number (number of protons)
  • Horizontal rows are called periods; vertical columns are called groups or families
  • Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties
  • The atomic symbol (1-2 letters) represents each element uniquely
  • Metals are found on the left side; nonmetals on the right side
  • The periodic table predicts electron configuration and chemical behavior
  • Group numbers indicate the number of valence electrons for main group elements

Reading the Periodic Table:

Atomic Number (top) = Number of protons (defines the element)

Element Symbol (center) = 1-2 letter abbreviation

Atomic Mass (bottom) = Average mass of all isotopes (in amu)

Example - Carbon (C):

Atomic Number = 6 | Symbol = C | Atomic Mass = 12.011 amu

Example: Using the Periodic Table

Problem: An element has 17 protons. What is the element, and how many electrons does a neutral atom have?

Solution:

‚Ä¢ Find atomic number 17 on the periodic table ‚Üí Chlorine (Cl)

‚Ä¢ In a neutral atom, # electrons = # protons = 17 electrons

‚Ä¢ Chlorine is in Group 17 (halogens) and is a nonmetal

‚ö° Section 4: Ions

Ions

Ions are atoms or groups of atoms that have gained or lost electrons, resulting in a net electrical charge. Cations are positive ions (lost electrons), while anions are negative ions (gained electrons). Ion formation is fundamental to understanding chemical bonding and compound formation.

Key Concepts:

  • Cations are positively charged ions formed when atoms lose electrons
  • Anions are negatively charged ions formed when atoms gain electrons
  • Metals typically form cations; nonmetals typically form anions
  • Ion charge = # protons - # electrons
  • Many main group elements form predictable charges based on their group number
  • The octet rule drives ion formation: atoms gain or lose electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons
  • Ion notation: Element symbol with charge as superscript (e.g., Na‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª, Ca¬≤‚Å∫)

üîÑ Section 5: Isotopes

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons, resulting in different mass numbers. While isotopes have identical chemical properties, they differ in mass and nuclear stability.

Key Concepts:

  • Isotopes have the same atomic number but different mass numbers
  • All isotopes of an element have identical chemical properties
  • Isotopes differ in mass and nuclear stability
  • Isotope notation: Mass number before element symbol (e.g., Carbon-12, ¬π¬≤C)
  • Nuclear symbol shows both atomic and mass numbers: ·¥¨ùöâX where A = mass number, Z = atomic number
  • Most elements exist as mixtures of two or more isotopes in nature
  • Some isotopes are radioactive (unstable nuclei)

Atomic Mass

The atomic mass (or atomic weight) of an element is the weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes of that element, measured in atomic mass units (amu). This value accounts for both the mass and relative abundance of each isotope.

Key Concepts:

  • Atomic mass is a weighted average based on isotope abundance
  • Most elements exist as mixtures of isotopes in nature
  • The atomic mass shown on the periodic table reflects natural isotope ratios
  • Atomic mass = Œ£ (isotope mass √ó fractional abundance)
  • Atomic mass units (amu) are defined relative to carbon-12
  • More abundant isotopes contribute more to the average atomic mass
  • Atomic mass is NOT the same as mass number (which is always a whole number)
