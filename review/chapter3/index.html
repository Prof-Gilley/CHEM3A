Section 1: Electromagnetic Radiation & Light

What is Electromagnetic Radiation?

Electromagnetic radiation is energy that travels through space as waves. All electromagnetic radiation travels at the speed of light (c = 3.00 √ó 10‚Å∏ m/s) but differs in wavelength and frequency. The electromagnetic spectrum includes radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

  • Frequency (ŒΩ): The number of wave cycles that pass a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz) or s‚Åª¬π
  • Inverse relationship: As wavelength increases, frequency decreases (and vice versa)
  • Energy and frequency: Higher frequency radiation carries more energy per photon
  • Visible light: Represents only a tiny portion of the electromagnetic spectrum (400-700 nm)
  • Color and wavelength: Violet light has the shortest wavelength (~400 nm) and highest energy in visible spectrum; red light has the longest wavelength (~700 nm) and lowest energy

Section 2: Atomic Models (Bohr vs. Quantum-Mechanical)

Two Models of the Atom:

Scientists have developed two major models to describe where electrons are located in atoms. The Bohr model (1913) describes electrons in fixed circular orbits, while the quantum-mechanical model (1920s) describes electrons in probability regions called orbitals. Both models help us understand atomic behavior, but the quantum-mechanical model is more accurate.

Bohr Model - Atoms with Orbits:

  • Fixed paths: Electrons travel in circular orbits at specific distances from the nucleus
  • Quantized energy: Only certain energy levels are allowed (n = 1, 2, 3...)
  • Emission spectra: When electrons drop from higher to lower energy levels, light is emitted
  • Absorption spectra: When electrons absorb energy, they jump to higher energy levels
  • Limitation: Only works well for hydrogen; fails for multi-electron atoms

Quantum-Mechanical Model - Atoms with Orbitals:

  • Probability regions: Electrons exist in 3D regions called orbitals, not fixed paths
  • Wave-particle duality: Electrons behave as both particles and waves
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty: Cannot know exact position and momentum simultaneously
  • Quantum numbers: Four quantum numbers (n, ‚Ñì, m‚Çó, m‚Çõ) describe each electron
  • Success: Accurately predicts behavior of all atoms, not just hydrogen

Comparing the Models:

Bohr Model: "The electron is in the third orbit" (like planets around the sun)

Quantum Model: "The electron is most likely found in the 3s orbital" (probability cloud)

Think of it this way: Bohr's model is like a race track with defined lanes, while the quantum model is like a probability map showing where you're most likely to find someone in a large room.

üí° Key Insight:

The Bohr model is simpler and great for understanding energy levels and spectroscopy. The quantum-mechanical model is more complex but necessary for understanding chemical bonding and the true behavior of electrons. You'll use both models depending on the question!

Section 3: Electron Configurations

What is Electron Configuration?

Electron configuration is a shorthand notation that shows how electrons are distributed among the various atomic orbitals. It uses numbers to indicate energy levels (shells), letters to indicate sublevel types (s, p, d, f), and superscripts to show how many electrons are in each sublevel. Example: Oxygen is 1s¬≤2s¬≤2p‚Å¥

Key Concepts:

  • Energy levels (n): Principal quantum number (n = 1, 2, 3, 4...) indicates the shell
  • Sublevels: s (holds 2e‚Åª), p (holds 6e‚Åª), d (holds 10e‚Åª), f (holds 14e‚Åª)
  • Aufbau principle: Fill orbitals in order of increasing energy (1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p...)
  • Reading from periodic table: The periodic table is organized by electron configuration blocks (s-block, p-block, d-block, f-block)
  • Shorthand notation: Use noble gas cores to abbreviate: [Ne]3s¬≤3p‚Åµ for chlorine instead of writing from 1s¬≤
  • Exceptions: Chromium and copper (and others) have slightly irregular configurations for added stability

Section 4: Orbital Diagrams

What are Orbital Diagrams?

Orbital diagrams (also called orbital filling diagrams or box diagrams) provide a visual representation of how electrons are arranged in orbitals. Each box represents one orbital, and arrows inside the boxes represent electrons with their spins. Arrows pointing up (‚Üë) and down (‚Üì) indicate opposite spins.

Three Rules for Filling Orbital Diagrams:

  • Aufbau Principle: Fill orbitals in order of increasing energy (same order as electron configurations)
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins
  • Hund's Rule: When filling orbitals of equal energy (like the three 2p orbitals), place one electron in each orbital before pairing any electrons
  • Why Hund's Rule? Electrons repel each other, so they prefer to be in separate orbitals (with the same spin) to minimize repulsion
  • Diagonal method: Visual way to remember fill order by following diagonals through s, p, d, f blocks

Section 5: Valence Electrons & Lewis Dot Structures

What are Valence Electrons?

Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost energy level (highest n value) of an atom. These are the electrons that participate in chemical bonding. Core electrons are all the inner electrons that are not valence electrons. For main-group elements, the number of valence electrons equals the group number (with some adjustments).

Key Concepts:

  • Finding valence electrons: For main-group elements, look at the group number. Groups 1-2 have 1-2 valence e‚Åª; Groups 13-18 have 3-8 valence e‚Åª
  • Lewis dot structures: Show only valence electrons as dots around the element symbol
  • Octet rule: Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons (like noble gases)
  • Cations (positive ions): Form when atoms lose valence electrons. Lewis structure has fewer dots
  • Anions (negative ions): Form when atoms gain valence electrons. Lewis structure has more dots (often with brackets and charge)
  • Pairing electrons: In Lewis structures, pair up electrons after placing one in each position (similar to Hund's rule)

Section 6: Periodic Trends

What are Periodic Trends?

Periodic trends are patterns in the properties of elements that repeat as you move across periods (rows) and down groups (columns) in the periodic table. These trends are directly related to electron configurations and how strongly the nucleus attracts electrons. Understanding these trends helps predict chemical behavior and reactivity.

Major Periodic Trends:

  • Atomic Radius: Size of the atom. INCREASES down a group (more shells), DECREASES across a period (stronger nuclear pull)
  • Ionic Radius: Cations are smaller than their atoms (lost electrons). Anions are larger than their atoms (gained electrons)
  • Ionization Energy: Energy needed to remove an electron. INCREASES across a period, DECREASES down a group
  • Electron Affinity: Energy change when an atom gains an electron. Generally becomes more negative (releases more energy) across a period
  • Metallic Character: Tendency to lose electrons and form cations. INCREASES down a group, DECREASES across a period
  • Effective Nuclear Charge: The net positive charge experienced by valence electrons. Increases across a period due to poor shielding by electrons in the same shell

Trend Directions - Quick Summary:

Atomic Radius: ‚Üë down groups | ‚Üê across periods (largest at bottom left)

Ionization Energy: ‚Üì down groups | ‚Üí across periods (highest at top right)

Electron Affinity: ‚Üì down groups | ‚Üí across periods (most negative at top right)

Metallic Character: ‚Üë down groups | ‚Üê across periods (most metallic at bottom left)

Note: Noble gases are exceptions to many trends due to their stable electron configurations

Example: Comparing Atomic Radii

Question: Arrange these elements in order of increasing atomic radius: F, Cl, Br

Solution:

1. All three are in Group 17 (halogens)

2. As you go down a group, atomic radius increases

3. F is smallest (Period 2), Cl is medium (Period 3), Br is largest (Period 4)

Answer: F < Cl < Br

Example: Comparing Ionization Energies

Question: Which has a higher first ionization energy: Na or Mg?

Solution:

1. Both are in Period 3

2. Moving left to right across a period, ionization energy increases

3. Mg has more protons ‚Üí stronger nuclear attraction ‚Üí harder to remove electron

Answer: Mg has higher ionization energy than Na

üéØ Memory Trick:

For atomic radius, think "bigger down, smaller across": Adding shells makes atoms bigger going down. More protons (with no new shells) pulls electrons tighter going across, making atoms smaller.

For ionization energy, think "harder to remove when held tighter": Small atoms with high nuclear charge hold electrons tightly, requiring more energy to remove them. That's why IE is highest at top right (except noble gases).
