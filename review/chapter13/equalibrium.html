1. Collision Theory and Reaction Rates

What Makes Reactions Happen?

For a chemical reaction to occur, particles must collide with:

  • Sufficient energy - Must meet or exceed the activation energy (Ea)
  • Proper orientation - Molecules must be aligned correctly

Factors Affecting Reaction Rates:

1. Concentration

More particles = more collisions per unit time

2. Temperature

Higher T = faster moving particles = more energetic collisions

3. Surface Area

More exposed surface = more collision sites

4. Catalysts

Lower the activation energy required

üìù Example: Temperature Effects

At 25¬∞C, average particle speed ‚âà 500 m/s
At 50¬∞C, average particle speed ‚âà 550 m/s
Result: More particles exceed activation energy threshold ‚Üí faster reaction

2. Dynamic Equilibrium

What is Equilibrium?

Chemical equilibrium occurs when the forward and reverse reactions proceed at equal rates. At this point:

  • Macroscopic properties (concentrations, color, pressure) remain constant
  • Both forward and reverse reactions continue - equilibrium is dynamic, not static!
  • Can be approached from either direction
  • Requires a closed system

Reversible Reactions

Consider the reaction between hydrogen and iodine:

H‚ÇÇ(g) + I‚ÇÇ(g) ‚áå 2HI(g)

The double arrow (‚áå) indicates this is a reversible reaction. Both the forward reaction (forming HI) and reverse reaction (decomposing HI) occur simultaneously.

Reaching Equilibrium:

Initially:

  • Forward rate is fast (lots of reactants)
  • Reverse rate is slow (few products)

As reaction proceeds:

  • Forward rate decreases (fewer reactants)
  • Reverse rate increases (more products)

At equilibrium:

  • Rateforward = Ratereverse
  • Concentrations stop changing (but reactions continue!)

For a general reaction:

5. Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle:

"When a chemical system at equilibrium is disturbed by a stress, the system will respond in order to relieve the stress."

Types of stresses that affect equilibrium:

1. Concentration Changes

Adding or removing reactants or products

2. Temperature Changes

Heating or cooling the system

3. Pressure/Volume Changes

For gas-phase reactions only

Effect of Concentration Changes

General Rules:

  • ‚Üí Add reactant or remove product ‚Üí shifts RIGHT (toward products)
  • ‚Üê Add product or remove reactant ‚Üí shifts LEFT (toward reactants)

Example: The Haber-Bosch Process

Add N‚ÇÇ:

Equilibrium shifts RIGHT ‚Üí more NH‚ÇÉ produced

Equilibrium shifts RIGHT ‚Üí more NH‚ÇÉ produced to replace it

Equilibrium shifts LEFT ‚Üí more N‚ÇÇ and H‚ÇÇ produced

üí° Industrial Application:

In the Haber-Bosch process, NH‚ÇÉ is continuously removed from the system. This shifts equilibrium to the right, maximizing ammonia production!
