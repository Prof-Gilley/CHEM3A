1. Solutions - Homogeneous Mixtures

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Solvent: The substance that doesn't change phase or is the major component (usually water)
  • Solute: The substance that changes phase or is the minor component
  • "Like dissolves like": Polar solvents dissolve polar solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes
  • Intermolecular forces determine solubility

How Solutions Form

Solutions form when solute particles are surrounded by solvent molecules through:

  • Ion-dipole forces: For ionic compounds in polar solvents
  • Hydrogen bonding: For polar molecules with H-O, H-N, or H-F bonds
  • Dipole-dipole forces: For polar molecules
  • London dispersion forces: For nonpolar molecules

2. Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Strong electrolytes: Ionic compounds that dissociate completely (e.g., NaCl, KBr)
  • Weak electrolytes: Compounds that partially dissociate (e.g., acetic acid)
  • Nonelectrolytes: Molecular compounds that don't dissociate (e.g., sugar, ethanol)
  • Electrolytes conduct electricity due to mobile ions in solution

💡 Example

When NaCl dissolves in water:

NaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

The ions are hydrated (surrounded by water molecules) and can move freely, conducting electricity.

Solubility Rules

Important solubility generalizations:

  • Group 1 (alkali metals) and NH₄⁺ compounds are always soluble
  • Nitrate (NO₃⁻) and acetate (C₂H₃O₂⁻) compounds are always soluble
  • Most halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are soluble except with Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺
  • Most sulfates (SO₄²⁻) are soluble except with Ca²⁺, Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Pb²⁺
  • Most hydroxides (OH⁻) are insoluble except Group 1 and Ca²⁺, Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺
  • Most carbonates (CO₃²⁻), phosphates (PO₄³⁻), and sulfides (S²⁻) are insoluble except Group 1 and NH₄⁺

3. Solution Concentration - Molarity

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Molarity (M): moles of solute per liter of solution
  • Most common concentration unit in chemistry
  • Used as a conversion factor between moles and volume
  • Remember: volume of solution, not just solvent!
Molarity Formula:
M = moles of solute / liters of solution

or

M = n / V

💡 Example

Calculate the molarity of a solution containing 42.23 g NH₄Cl in 500.0 mL solution:

  1. Convert grams to moles: 42.23 g ÷ 53.50 g/mol = 0.7893 mol
  2. Convert mL to L: 500.0 mL = 0.5000 L
  3. Calculate molarity: M = 0.7893 mol / 0.5000 L = 1.579 M

Other Concentration Units

  • Percent by mass: (mass solute / mass solution) × 100%
  • Percent by volume: (volume solute / volume solution) × 100%
  • Molality (m): moles solute / kg solvent (used for colligative properties)

4. Solution Dilution

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Dilution: Adding solvent to decrease concentration
  • Moles of solute remain constant during dilution
  • Volume increases, molarity decreases
  • Stock solution: Concentrated solution used to prepare dilute solutions
Dilution Formula:
M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

Where:
M₁ = initial molarity
V₁ = initial volume
M₂ = final molarity
V₂ = final volume

💡 Example

How much 16 M HNO₃ is needed to make 8.00 L of 0.50 M solution?

Solution:

  1. Use M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
  2. (16 M)(V₁) = (0.50 M)(8.00 L)
  3. V₁ = 0.25 L = 250 mL

Take 250 mL of 16 M HNO₃ and dilute with water to 8.00 L total volume.

5. Using Molarity in Stoichiometry

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Molarity serves as a conversion factor between moles and volume
  • Apply stoichiometry principles to solution reactions
  • Titration: Technique to determine unknown concentration
  • Combine mole ratios with molarity calculations

Titration Applications

  • Determining concentration of acid/base solutions
  • Finding purity of samples
  • Analyzing composition of mixtures

💡 Example: Titration

What volume of 3.44 M HCl will react with 5.33 mol of CaCO₃?

Reaction: 2HCl + CaCO₃ → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

Solution:

  1. Use mole ratio: 5.33 mol CaCO₃ × (2 mol HCl / 1 mol CaCO₃) = 10.66 mol HCl
  2. Convert to volume: 10.66 mol ÷ 3.44 M = 3.10 L

6. Colligative Properties

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Colligative properties: Properties that depend only on the number of solute particles, not their identity
  • Four main colligative properties: vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure
  • van't Hoff factor (i): Number of particles a compound produces when dissolved
  • Ionic compounds have i > 1; molecular compounds have i = 1

van't Hoff Factor (i)

  • NaCl: i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
  • CaCl₂: i = 3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻)
  • Al₂(SO₄)₃: i = 5 (2Al³⁺ + 3SO₄²⁻)
  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆): i = 1 (stays as molecules)

💡 Example

Compare freezing points: 0.1 m NaCl, 0.1 m C₆H₁₂O₆, 0.1 m CaI₂

  • NaCl: 0.1 m × 2 = 0.2 m effective concentration
  • C₆H₁₂O₆: 0.1 m × 1 = 0.1 m effective concentration
  • CaI₂: 0.1 m × 3 = 0.3 m effective concentration

Order (lowest to highest freezing point): CaI₂ < NaCl < C₆H₁₂O₆

7. Freezing Point Depression & Boiling Point Elevation

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Solutions have higher boiling points than pure solvents
  • Solutions have lower freezing points than pure solvents
  • Change depends on molality (m) and van't Hoff factor (i)
  • Real-world applications: Antifreeze, salting icy roads, cooking
Boiling Point Elevation:
ΔTb = Kb × m × i

Kb (water) = 0.512 °C/m

Freezing Point Depression:
ΔTf = Kf × m × i

Kf (water) = 1.86 °C/m

💡 Example: Antifreeze

What is the concentration of ethylene glycol needed to lower the freezing point by 2.64°C?

Solution:

  1. ΔTf = Kf × m × i
  2. 2.64°C = (1.86 °C/m) × m × 1
  3. m = 2.64 / 1.86 = 1.42 m

💡 Example: Salt Water Boiling

10.0 g NaCl in 100.0 g water. What's the boiling point?

Solution:

  1. Molality: (10.0 g / 58.45 g/mol) / 0.100 kg = 1.71 m
  2. ΔTb = 0.512 °C/m × 1.71 m × 2 = 1.75°C
  3. New boiling point: 100.00°C + 1.75°C = 101.75°C

8. Osmotic Pressure

🎯 Key Concepts

  • Osmosis: Movement of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from low to high solute concentration
  • Semipermeable membrane: Allows solvent but not solute to pass through
  • Osmotic pressure (Π): Pressure needed to prevent osmosis
  • Biological importance: IV fluids, cell function, plant water transport
Osmotic Pressure Formula:
Π = MRT

Where:
Π = osmotic pressure (atm)
M = molarity (mol/L)
R = 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)
T = temperature (K)

Biological Applications

  • Isotonic solutions: Same concentration as body fluids (0.9% NaCl)
  • Hypertonic solutions: Higher concentration than cells → cells shrink
  • Hypotonic solutions: Lower concentration than cells → cells swell
  • IV fluids: Must be isotonic to prevent cell damage
  • Why you can't drink seawater: Hypertonic - pulls water from cells

💡 Example

Calculate osmotic pressure of 0.333 M C₆H₁₂O₆ at 25°C:

Solution:

  1. Convert temperature: T = 25 + 273 = 298 K
  2. Π = MRT
  3. Π = (0.333 M)(0.08206 L·atm/mol·K)(298 K)
  4. Π = 8.14 atm

This is equivalent to a column of water 84 m tall!
