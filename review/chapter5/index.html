Section 1: Avogadro's Number & Mole-Atom Conversions

What is a Mole?

A mole is the SI unit for amount of substance. One mole contains exactly 6.022 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or formula units). This number is called Avogadro's Number (N‚Çê). The mole allows us to count atoms and molecules by weighing them!

Key Points

  • 1 mole = 6.022 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ particles (Avogadro's Number)
  • The mole is a counting unit, like a dozen (12) or a gross (144)
  • It bridges the microscopic world (atoms) with the macroscopic world (grams)
  • Use dimensional analysis to convert between moles and atoms
  • Avogadro's Number is the same for ALL substances
  • Think of it as the "chemist's dozen" for counting particles

Section 2: Molar Mass & Mole-Mass Conversions

What is Molar Mass?

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in grams per mole (g/mol). It's numerically equal to the atomic or molecular mass, but the units are g/mol instead of amu. Molar mass is your conversion factor between grams and moles!

Key Points

  • Molar mass has units of g/mol (grams per mole)
  • For elements, molar mass equals atomic mass from the periodic table
  • For compounds, add up all atomic masses from the formula
  • Use the periodic table to find atomic masses
  • Molar mass is the bridge between mass (grams) and amount (moles)
  • Always include units in your calculations to avoid errors

moles = mass (g) √∑ molar mass (g/mol)

Section 3: Mass and Particle Conversions

Combining Conversions

Now we combine our two conversion skills! To convert between mass (grams) and number of particles (atoms, molecules, formula units), we must go through moles as an intermediate step. Think of moles as the central hub connecting all quantity measurements in chemistry.

Key Points

  • Mass ‚Üî Moles ‚Üî Particles: Always go through moles!
  • Use molar mass to convert between grams and moles
  • Use Avogadro's Number to convert between moles and particles
  • Two-step problems: grams ‚Üí moles ‚Üí particles (or reverse)
  • Dimensional analysis is your friend - units must cancel properly
  • Set up conversion factors so unwanted units cancel out

Solution:

Step 1: 44.0 g CO‚ÇÇ √ó (1 mol/44.01 g) = 1.00 mol CO‚ÇÇ

Step 2: 1.00 mol √ó (6.022 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ molecules/mol) = 6.02 √ó 10¬≤¬≥ molecules CO‚ÇÇ

Section 4: Percent Composition

What is Percent Composition?

Percent composition tells you the percentage by mass of each element in a compound. It answers the question: "What fraction of this compound's mass comes from each element?" This is useful for analyzing unknown compounds and checking purity of samples.

Key Points

  • Percent composition = (mass of element / total mass of compound) √ó 100%
  • Calculate using molar masses from the chemical formula
  • All percentages in a compound should add up to 100%
  • Percent composition is the same for any sample size of a pure compound
  • Used in experimental analysis to identify unknown compounds
  • Can work backwards: percent composition ‚Üí empirical formula

Percent Composition Formula

% Element = (mass of element in 1 mol compound / molar mass of compound) √ó 100%
% Element = (n √ó atomic mass / formula mass) √ó 100%

where n = subscript of element in formula

Example Problem

Question: Find the percent composition of water, H‚ÇÇO.

Solution:

Molar mass H‚ÇÇO = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 g/mol

% H = [2(1.008) / 18.016] √ó 100% = 11.19% H

% O = [16.00 / 18.016] √ó 100% = 88.81% O

Check: 11.19% + 88.81% = 100.00% ‚úì

‚úì Check Your Work: All percent compositions in a compound must sum to 100% (allowing for small rounding errors). If they don't, recheck your calculations!

Section 5: Empirical Formulas & Hydrates

Empirical vs Molecular Formulas

An empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound (e.g., CH‚ÇÇO). A molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms (e.g., C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ for glucose). The empirical formula is found from experimental data like percent composition. Hydrates are compounds that contain water molecules in their crystal structure.

Key Points

  • Empirical formula = simplest whole-number ratio of elements
  • Start with percent composition or mass data for each element
  • Convert masses to moles, then divide by the smallest number of moles
  • If you get non-whole numbers, multiply all by the same factor
  • Hydrates contain water (H‚ÇÇO) as part of their crystal structure
  • Hydrate formula: Compound ¬∑ nH‚ÇÇO (e.g., CuSO‚ÇÑ¬∑5H‚ÇÇO)
  • Calculate percent water in hydrate for purity analysis

Empirical Formula Steps

2. grams ‚Üí moles (divide by molar mass)
3. moles ‚Üí ratio (divide all by smallest)
4. ratio ‚Üí whole numbers (multiply if needed)

Solution:
