Section 1: Properties and Changes of Matter

Physical vs. Chemical Properties & Changes

Physical properties can be observed without changing the substance's identity (color, melting point, density). Chemical properties describe how a substance reacts or changes into different substances (flammability, reactivity with acids). Physical changes alter appearance but not composition (melting ice), while chemical changes produce new substances with different properties (burning wood).

Key Points

  • Physical properties: observable without chemical change (color, state, melting point, density, hardness)
  • Chemical properties: describe how substances react or change (flammability, corrosiveness, reactivity)
  • Physical changes: reversible, no new substances formed (state changes, dissolving, shape changes)
  • Chemical changes: new substances formed, often irreversible (combustion, rusting, cooking eggs)
  • Law of Conservation of Mass: mass is neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions
  • Signs of chemical change: color change, gas production, temperature change, precipitate formation

Conservation of Mass

Mass of Reactants = Mass of Products
Total mass before = Total mass after

Example Problem

Question: Identify whether each is physical or chemical: (a) ice melting (b) wood burning (c) sugar dissolving

Solution:

(a) Physical - water is still H‚ÇÇO, just different state

(b) Chemical - wood becomes ash, CO‚ÇÇ, H‚ÇÇO (new substances)

(c) Physical - sugar molecules unchanged, just dispersed in water

Section 2: Compounds and Chemical Formulas

What Are Compounds?

A compound is a pure substance made of two or more different elements chemically bonded together in fixed proportions. Unlike mixtures, compounds have unique properties different from their constituent elements. Chemical formulas use element symbols and subscripts to show the types and numbers of atoms in a compound (e.g., H‚ÇÇO has 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom).

Key Points

  • Compounds form when elements combine chemically in fixed ratios
  • Compounds have properties totally different from their elements (e.g., NaCl vs. Na and Cl‚ÇÇ)
  • Chemical formulas show element symbols and subscript numbers
  • Subscripts tell how many atoms of each element are present
  • Molecular compounds: discrete molecules (H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, CH‚ÇÑ)
  • Ionic compounds: arrays of ions in crystal lattices (NaCl, CaCl‚ÇÇ, MgO)
  • Formula unit: smallest ratio of ions in an ionic compound

Reading Chemical Formulas

Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 1 Ca, 2 O, 2 H atoms
Al‚ÇÇ(SO‚ÇÑ)‚ÇÉ ‚Üí 2 Al, 3 S, 12 O atoms

Section 3: Ionic Compounds and Ions

Ions and Ionic Compounds

Ions are atoms or groups of atoms with electric charge. Cations are positive ions (formed by losing electrons), typically metals. Anions are negative ions (formed by gaining electrons), typically nonmetals. Ionic compounds form when cations and anions combine in ratios that produce zero net charge. Polyatomic ions are charged groups of atoms bonded together (like SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫).

Key Points

  • Monatomic ions: single atoms with charge (Na‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, O¬≤‚Åª)
  • Polyatomic ions: groups of atoms with overall charge (SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫, CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª)
  • Metals form cations (positive) by losing electrons
  • Nonmetals form anions (negative) by gaining electrons
  • Writing formulas: balance charges to get zero net charge
  • Use the "criss-cross" method: charges become subscripts
  • Reduce subscripts to lowest whole number ratio

Section 4: Naming Ionic Compounds

Ionic Nomenclature Rules

Type I ionic compounds contain metals that form only one charge (like Na‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Al¬≥‚Å∫). Name: [metal name] + [nonmetal root]-ide. Type II ionic compounds contain metals with variable charges (like Fe¬≤‚Å∫/Fe¬≥‚Å∫, Cu‚Å∫/Cu¬≤‚Å∫). Name: [metal name](Roman numeral) + [nonmetal root]-ide. For polyatomic ions, keep their names as-is (like sulfate, nitrate, carbonate).

Key Points

  • Type I metals: Groups 1, 2, Al, Zn, Ag, Cd - form only one charge
  • Type II metals: Transition metals and some post-transition - variable charges
  • Type I naming: Just metal name + nonmetal-ide (NaCl = sodium chloride)
  • Type II naming: Metal(charge) + nonmetal-ide (FeCl‚ÇÉ = iron(III) chloride)
  • With polyatomic ions: keep polyatomic name (CaSO‚ÇÑ = calcium sulfate)
  • Common polyatomic ions: SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª (sulfate), NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª (nitrate), OH‚Åª (hydroxide), CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª (carbonate)
  • NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ (ammonium) is positive - only common polyatomic cation!

FeCl‚ÇÉ: Cl‚Åª = -1, need 3 ‚Üí Total negative = -3

Section 5: Naming Molecular Compounds

Molecular Compound Nomenclature

Molecular (covalent) compounds form between two nonmetals sharing electrons. Naming uses Greek prefixes to indicate the number of each atom. Format: [prefix]-[first element] [prefix]-[second element]-ide. The prefix "mono-" is omitted for the first element but used for the second if there's only one atom. Common exceptions: H‚ÇÇO (water), NH‚ÇÉ (ammonia), CH‚ÇÑ (methane).

Key Points

  • Greek prefixes: mono(1), di(2), tri(3), tetra(4), penta(5), hexa(6), hepta(7), octa(8), nona(9), deca(10)
  • First element: use prefix only if >1 atom (CO not monocarbon monoxide)
  • Second element: always use prefix + change ending to "-ide"
  • CO‚ÇÇ = carbon dioxide (not monocarbon dioxide)
  • N‚ÇÇO‚ÇÑ = dinitrogen tetraoxide (both get prefixes)
  • Drop "a" or "o" before vowels: "tetraoxide" not "tetraaoxide"
  • These rules DON'T apply to ionic compounds - only molecular!

Greek Prefix Reference

1=mono, 2=di, 3=tri, 4=tetra, 5=penta
6=hexa, 7=hepta, 8=octa, 9=nona, 10=deca
PCl‚ÇÖ = phosphorus pentachloride

Example Problem

Question: Name these molecular compounds: (a) SO‚ÇÉ (b) N‚ÇÇO (c) CCl‚ÇÑ

Solution:

(a) sulfur trioxide (1 S = no prefix, 3 O = tri)

(b) dinitrogen monoxide (2 N = di, 1 O = mono)

(c) carbon tetrachloride (1 C = no prefix, 4 Cl = tetra)

Section 6: Naming Acids & Nomenclature Summary

Acids and Complete Nomenclature

Acids are compounds that release H‚Å∫ ions in water. Binary acids contain hydrogen and one other nonmetal (HCl, HBr): named "hydro[nonmetal]ic acid". Oxyacids contain hydrogen, oxygen, and another element (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, HNO‚ÇÉ). If the polyatomic ion ends in "-ate", change to "-ic acid". If it ends in "-ite", change to "-ous acid". Master all three naming systems (ionic, molecular, acids) to succeed in chemistry!

  • Oxyacids from -ate ions: [root]ic acid (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ from SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª sulfate = sulfuric acid)
  • Oxyacids from -ite ions: [root]ous acid (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÉ from SO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª sulfite = sulfurous acid)
