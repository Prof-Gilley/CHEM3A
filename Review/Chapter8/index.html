Why Study Gases?

Of the three basic phases of matter‚Äîsolids, liquids, and gases‚Äîonly gases have predictable physical properties that are independent of their chemical identity. Whether it's helium, oxygen, or sulfur vapors, gases behave similarly under the same conditions.

Key Characteristics of Gases:

  • No definite shape or volume - gases fill whatever container they're in
  • Highly compressible and expandable - can be squeezed or expanded significantly
  • Extremely low densities - about 1/1000th the density of liquids or solids
  • Mix spontaneously - form homogeneous solutions (like air: mostly N‚ÇÇ and O‚ÇÇ)
What You'll Learn:
  • Kinetic Molecular Theory - how gas particles behave at the molecular level
  • Gas Laws - mathematical relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and amount
  • The Ideal Gas Law - a powerful equation connecting all gas properties
  • Gas Mixtures - how different gases interact when combined

8.1 Solids, Liquids, and Gases - A Molecular Comparison
‚ñº
COR Connection: Understand how intermolecular forces influence physical properties (Course Objective #8)

Kinetic Molecular Description

The physical state of matter depends on the balance between kinetic energy (keeps molecules apart and moving) and intermolecular forces (tries to draw particles together).

Properties of Gases:

  • Widely separated molecules with lots of empty space
  • Kinetic energy > attractive forces between molecules
  • No significant attractive forces allow gas to expand and fill container
  • If attractive forces become large enough, gases show non-ideal behavior

Properties of Liquids:

  • Intermolecular forces strong enough to hold molecules close together
  • More dense and less compressible than gases
  • Definite volume, independent of container size/shape
  • Molecules free to move past or slide over one another - can be poured

Properties of Solids:

  • Intermolecular forces strong enough to lock molecules in position
  • Not compressible due to lack of space between molecules
  • If molecules adopt highly ordered packing: crystalline
  • Rigid due to strong intermolecular forces
Key Takeaway: Temperature affects kinetic energy; pressure affects how close molecules are. Changing these conditions can cause phase changes.
8.2 Pressure - The Result of Constant Molecular Collisions
‚ñº
COR Connection: Apply dimensional analysis and unit conversions (Course Objective #3)

What is Pressure?

Pressure (P) is the force exerted by gas particles colliding with container walls, distributed over the wall's area:

pressure = force / area

Units of Pressure:

  • Pascal (Pa): SI unit = 1 N/m¬≤ (often too small to be practical)
  • Atmosphere (atm): standard atmospheric pressure at sea level
  • Millimeters of mercury (mmHg): pressure from 1 mm column of Hg
  • Torr: equivalent to 1 mmHg
Important Equivalents:
1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 101,325 Pa

Practice: Converting Pressure Units

Example: Convert 595 torr to atmospheres:

595 torr √ó (1 atm / 760 torr) = 0.783 atm
8.3 Kinetic Molecular Theory - A Model for Gases
‚ñº
COR Connection: Use fundamental laws, principles, and theories to predict gas behavior (Course Objective #8)

The Five Principles of Kinetic Molecular Theory:

  • Gases consist of tiny particles in constant motion
  • Collisions are elastic - no net energy loss
  • Large distances between particles - particle size is tiny compared to separation
  • No interactive forces between gas particles (no attraction or repulsion)
  • Average speed depends on temperature - higher T means faster movement
What This Means:
  • Most of a gas is empty space ‚Üí low density, easily compressed
  • Constant motion ‚Üí gases always mix completely
  • Elastic collisions ‚Üí pressure remains constant if conditions don't change

Ideal vs. Real Gases

An ideal gas perfectly follows kinetic molecular theory. Real gases deviate slightly, but the theory is still highly accurate for most conditions.

Combining the Simple Gas Laws

When MORE THAN ONE variable changes (but amount stays constant), use the Combined Gas Law:

P‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ/T‚ÇÅ = P‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ/T‚ÇÇ

How to Use It:

  • Identify initial conditions (P‚ÇÅ, V‚ÇÅ, T‚ÇÅ)
  • Identify final conditions (P‚ÇÇ, V‚ÇÇ, T‚ÇÇ)
  • Temperature MUST be in Kelvin
  • Rearrange algebraically to solve for unknown variable
  • Units for P and V must match on both sides
Pro Tip: The Combined Gas Law includes Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's Laws. If one variable stays constant, it cancels out!

8.5 The Ideal Gas Law and Applications
‚ñº
COR Connection: Apply ideal gas law to stoichiometry, density, and molar volumes (Course Objectives #3, #8, #9)

The Ideal Gas Law

The most important gas equation relates ALL FOUR independent properties:

PV = nRT

Where:

  • P = pressure (atm, torr, Pa, etc.)
  • V = volume (L)
  • n = moles of gas
  • R = ideal gas constant
  • T = temperature (MUST be in Kelvin)

Values of R (Gas Constant):

  • R = 0.08206 L¬∑atm/(mol¬∑K)
  • R = 62.36 L¬∑torr/(mol¬∑K)
  • R = 8.314 J/(mol¬∑K)
Key Advantage: The Ideal Gas Law doesn't require comparing two sets of conditions - you can calculate any property if you know the other three!

Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP)

  • T = 0¬∞C = 273 K
  • P = 1 atm
  • Molar Volume at STP = 22.4 L/mol

Applications:

  • Calculate density of gases
  • Determine molar mass from gas measurements
  • Stoichiometry with gaseous reactants/products
  • Gas mixture calculations

8.6 Mixtures of Gases - Dalton's Law
‚ñº
COR Connection: Apply gas laws to gas mixtures and laboratory applications (Course Objectives #8, #11)

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

In a gas mixture, each gas exerts pressure independently. The total pressure equals the sum of all partial pressures:

Ptotal = P‚ÇÅ + P‚ÇÇ + P‚ÇÉ + ...

Partial Pressure

The partial pressure is the contribution one gas makes to total pressure in a mixture.

Example: Air is ~78% N‚ÇÇ and ~21% O‚ÇÇ

  • If atmospheric pressure = 1.00 atm
  • PN‚ÇÇ = 0.78 atm
  • PO‚ÇÇ = 0.21 atm
  • Ptotal = 0.78 + 0.21 = 0.99 atm (other gases make up remaining 0.01 atm)

Collecting Gases Over Water

Common laboratory technique:

  • Gas is collected in tube filled with water
  • Gas displaces the water as it's produced
  • Collected gas is mixed with water vapor
  • Must account for vapor pressure of water
Pgas = Ptotal - PH‚ÇÇO
Lab Application: Always subtract water vapor pressure from total pressure to get the actual pressure of your collected gas!

Bonus Gas Laws and Empirical Formulas
‚ñº

Using Gas Laws to Determine Molecular Formulas

The Ideal Gas Law can help determine the molar mass and empirical formula of unknown gases:

Method:

  • Measure mass of gas sample
  • Measure P, V, and T conditions
  • Use PV = nRT to find moles (n)
  • Calculate molar mass = mass/moles
  • Use molar mass to determine molecular formula
Application: This technique connects stoichiometry, empirical formulas, and gas laws together - a common exam topic!

Success Strategies for Chapter 8

Before You Start Any Problem:
  • Convert temperature to Kelvin (K = ¬∞C + 273)
  • Make sure pressure units match
  • Make sure volume units match
  • Identify which variables are constant vs. changing

Choosing the Right Equation:

  • Only one set of conditions? ‚Üí Use Ideal Gas Law (PV = nRT)
  • Gas mixture? ‚Üí Use Dalton's Law
  • At STP? ‚Üí Use 22.4 L/mol molar volume

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Forgetting to convert ¬∞C to K
  • Using different units for P‚ÇÅ and P‚ÇÇ
  • Forgetting to subtract water vapor pressure
  • Using wrong value of R for your pressure units
